Kansas City is a strong, diversified market, but it has a structural quirk no other metro DealBrief covers shares to the same degree: it sits on a state line. A building in Kansas City, Missouri and one across the line in Kansas or in Johnson County are taxed under entirely different systems. Get the state and county right before anything else.

## Missouri: 19% residential ratio, biennial reassessment

On the Missouri side, residential property (including apartments) is assessed at **19% of market value**, and counties reassess in **odd-numbered years**. Jackson County (most of Kansas City, MO), Clay, Platte, and Cass counties all follow this. The effective rate on Jackson County multifamily runs near **1.50%** of market value depending on the taxing district.

The wrinkle is recent history. **Jackson County's 2023 reassessment was chaotic**: some assessments spiked far beyond market, the Missouri State Tax Commission ordered rollbacks, and litigation followed. The practical lesson for a buyer is that the county's assessed value on a Jackson County parcel may be unreliable or under appeal, and the seller's tax bill may not reflect where the number ultimately lands. Verify the current assessed value directly and understand whether it is under appeal.

## Kansas: different ratio, higher rates

Across the state line, Kansas assesses residential at **11.5%** but multifamily is treated differently, and the effective rates in Wyandotte County (Kansas City, KS) run near **1.79%**, among the highest in the metro, while Johnson County (Overland Park, Olathe) runs near **1.34%**. Same metro, very different tax outcomes. Which side of State Line Road the parcel sits on is a first-order underwriting question.

## Verify the assessed value, not just the rate

Because of the reassessment turmoil on the Missouri side and the ratio differences across the line, the single most important Kansas City diligence step is confirming the current, correct assessed value for the exact parcel and jurisdiction, then applying the right ratio and rate. Do not underwrite off the seller's stale bill.

## Permits and the renovation story

Major work requires a permit through the relevant city (Kansas City, MO has its own system; KCK, Overland Park, and Olathe each have theirs). Cross-reference "renovated" claims against the permit record. See the [full guide to checking permit history](/blog/check-multifamily-renovation-permits).

## The broker pitch, translated

- **"Taxes are $X"**: confirm the state, county, ratio, and whether a Jackson County value is under appeal.
- **"Great Kansas City location"**: confirm which side of the state line, which changes the tax system entirely.
- **"Renovated"**: verify permits for system work.
- **"Cap rate is X%"**: ask for the NOI math with the correct ratio and rate for the actual jurisdiction.

The Kansas City items above sit on top of the general [pre-offer due diligence checklist](/blog/pre-offer-due-diligence-checklist-small-multifamily): permit history, code violations, demographics, debt-service stress test, and FEMA flood zone (the Missouri and Kansas rivers put parts of the metro in mapped zones).
